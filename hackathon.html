Technical Services Hackathon

Utilizing Merkle-tree hashes to become CIS compliant

I joined SEI Investments in 2015, right after finishing my masters program. I was hired into the Graduates Rotational Program working on the Global Wealth Platform, which was intended to allow new hires the opportunity to float about he department and land in a group that attracted their interests. As I possessed a penchant for cybersecurity and systems management I naturally gravitated towards the technical services group. Out of the 20 new hires that were brought on to the GWP before me, I was the only one to end up in tech. services. It was soon after joining the team that I found out the probable causes of this phenomena.

The GWP was striving for CIS compliance which required a number of drastic changes around the department. Some of those being enhancing account management procedures, enforcing stricter physical access controls, and a little something called system hardening and continuous system vulnerability management. It turns out, when I was being interviewed I had mentioned that I was familiar with OpenVAS (Opensource Nessus), and several managers had kept that fact in mind. Needless to say, they were pleased to welcome me.

If you haven't checked out my crypto mining project yet, you might be missing some backstory here about my history with cryptography and blockchain theory.

Back to the story; close to the end of the year, development cycles tend to slow down with folks taking time off and the holidays. So the GWP department hosts a hackathon across all of the different release trains to attempt a year end productivity milking from their salaried employees with the promise of $25 Wawa gift cards.

For me it wasn't about a gift card, this was my chance to show off my understanding of cryptography in a practical, home-made, application that could save the department oodles and achieve the highly sought after "Continuous Monitoring" checkmark for the CIS compliance audit.
